The 47-Day Certificate Timeline: Understanding SC-081v3

Overview of SC-081v3

The Certification Authority/Browser Forum (CA/B Forum) recently passed Ballot SC-081v3, a landmark decision that fundamentally reshapes the lifecycle of publicly-trusted TLS certificates. This ballot mandates a phased reduction in certificate validity from the current 398 days down to a maximum of 47 days by 2029.

This shift isn't just a policy change; it's a technical mandate for automation. In an environment where certificates expire every 47 days, manual renewal processes—no matter how well-documented—become a significant operational risk and a guaranteed path to service outages.

The Phased Rollout Schedule

To give organizations time to adapt, the transition is divided into three distinct phases:

  • Phase 1: 200 Days (Effective March 15, 2026)
    Maximum validity drops from 398 to 200 days (effectively 199 days). Organizations will move from annual to semi-annual renewal cadences.
  • Phase 2: 100 Days (Effective March 15, 2027)
    Validity drops further to 100 days (effectively 99 days). This quarterly cadence is where manual processes typically begin to fail at scale.
  • Phase 3: 47 Days (Effective March 15, 2029)
    The final target of 47 days (effectively 46 days). This necessitates ~8 renewals per year per certificate.

The Hidden Constraint: 10-Day DCV

Alongside the 47-day limit, Domain Control Validation (DCV) reuse is being slashed to just 10 days. This means you must re-verify domain ownership for almost every single issuance, making DNS-01 or automated HTTP-01 validation mandatory.

Why 47 Days?

The number 47 was chosen carefully by browser vendors (primarily Apple and Google). It represents:

47 days = 31 days (max month) + 15 days (half month) + 1 day wiggle room.

This allows for a clean "monthly" renewal cycle. If you renew at the two-thirds mark (the industry standard), you'll renew around day 30 or 31, aligning with monthly maintenance windows and automated billing cycles.

Operational Readiness Checklist

To prepare for SC-081v3, Senior Security Engineers should focus on these immediate action items:

Automation Strategy

  • Standardize on ACME (Automated Certificate Management Environment) for all public endpoints.
  • Implement DNS-01 validation via API-driven DNS providers (AWS Route53, Cloudflare, etc.) to handle the 10-day DCV limit.
  • Deploy a Certificate Lifecycle Management (CLM) tool to gain 100% visibility into your cert inventory.

The Security Rationale

Browsers are pushing for shorter lifespans for several critical security reasons:

  • Broken Revocation: CRLs and OCSP are notoriously unreliable. Shorter validity periods act as a "natural revocation" mechanism.
  • Key Rotation: Forces regular rotation of private keys, limiting the window of exposure if a key is compromised.
  • Agility: Normalizes the process of replacing certificates, making it easier to respond to emergency events like Heartbleed or the transition to Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC).

In conclusion, the 47-day timeline is the end of "set it and forget it" PKI. For the modern CyberOps team, success in this new era is defined by the robustness of your automation pipelines.
